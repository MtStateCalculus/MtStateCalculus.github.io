A. With one step of \(\Delta t = 1\text{,}\) we have \(B(1) \approx 1000 + (1)(0.04)(1000) = 1040\text{.}\) B. With two steps of \(\Delta t = 0.5\text{,}\) we have \(B(0.5) \approx 1000 + (0.5)(0.04)(1000) = 1020\text{,}\) \(B(1) \approx 1020 + (0.5)(0.04)(1020) = 1040.4\text{.}\) Note that we can simplify this some: \(B(0.5) \approx (1 + \frac{0.04}{2}) (1000)\text{,}\) so that \(B(1) \approx B(0.5) + \frac{0.04}{2} B(0.5) = (1 + \frac{0.04}{2})^2 (1000)\text{.}\) C. With four steps of \(\Delta t = 0.25\text{,}\) we have, following the pattern we saw in (B), \(B(0.25) \approx 1000 + (0.25)(0.04)(1000) = (1 + \frac{0.04}{4}) (1000)\text{,}\) \(B(0.5) \approx B(0.25) + (0.25)(0.04) B(0.25) = (1 + \frac{0.04}{4})^2 (1000)\text{,}\) \(B(0.75) \approx B(0.5) + (0.25)(0.04) B(0.5) = (1 + \frac{0.04}{4})^3 (1000)\text{,}\) and \(B(1) \approx B(0.75) + (0.25)(0.04) B(0.75) = (1 + \frac{0.04}{4})^4 (1000) = 1040.604\text{.}\) D. In part (A), we get our approximation by making a single increment, \(\Delta B\text{,}\) where \(\Delta B\) is just \(0.04 B\text{.}\) If we think in terms of interest, \(\Delta B\) is just like getting one end of the year interest payment. Since \(\Delta B\) is 0.04 times the balance \(B\text{,}\) it is like getting 40 dollars. Part (B) is equivalent to computing the final amount in an account that begins with 1000 dollars and earns 40 dollars twice annually. Each step is like computing the interest after 6 months. When \(t=0.5\text{,}\) for example, the interest is \(\Delta B=(0.04 B)\cdot\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) and we add this to 1000 dollars to get the new balance. Similarly, part (C) is equivalent to the final amount in an account that has an initial balance of 1000 dollars and earns 40 dollars compounded quarterly.
