Because the graph of \(g'(x)\) is positive for all \(-2 \lt x \lt 2\text{,}\) we know that the graph of \(g(x)\) must always be increasing there. The derivative appears to be zero at \(x = \pm 2\text{,}\) so the graph of \(g(x)\) must start out flat at \(x = -2\text{,}\) and then increases more and more rapidly as it approaches \(x = 1\text{.}\) After \(x = 1\) the it increases less rapidly, finally flattening out entirely as we get to \(x = 2\text{.}\)

A. The graph has an inflection point at \(x = 1\text{,}\) where the slope changes from positive and increasing to positive and decreasing.

B. The function has a global maximum at \(x=2\) and a global minimum at \(x=-2\text{.}\)

C. Since the function is increasing over the interval \(-2 \lt x \lt 2\text{,}\) we know that \(g(0)\) must be greater than \(g(-2)\text{,}\) and so must be in the interval \((9,\infty)\text{.}\) (We note that it appears that the magnitude of the derivative \(g'\) is also less than one for the entire domain that we are considering, so we can actually give a smaller interval for the possible values of \(g\text{;}\) any interval between \((9,\infty)\) and \((9,11)\) is reasonable.)

D. Again, since the function is increasing over the interval \(-2 \lt x \lt 2\text{,}\) we know that \(g(-2) = 9 \lt g(0) \lt g(2)\text{.}\)
