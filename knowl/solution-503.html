Solution 8.4.6.1.1.

(a) For \(\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty} {(-1)^n\left({n+1\over n}\right)}\text{,}\) we have \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty} {n+1\over n} = 1\text{.}\) This means that the alternating series test doesn't tell us whether this series converges: if the limit had been zero, we would know that the series converged, but if it's non-zero the test doesn't help. However, note that \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}\left({n+1\over n}\right) = 1 \ne 0\text{.}\) Thus \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty} (-1)^n\left({n+1\over n}\right)\) doesn't exist, and we know that the series must in fact diverge. (b) For \(\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty} {(-1)^n\over 2^n}\text{,}\) we have \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty} {1\over 2^n} = 0\text{.}\) Thus, by the alternating series test, we know that this series converges (it is at least conditionally convergent). Because \(\sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty} {1\over 2^n}\) also converges (by comparison to a \(p\)-series or by the integral test), our original series must be not only conditionally convergent but absolutely convergent.
