\begin{equation*} g(x) = g(7) + g'(7)(x-7) + \frac{g''(7)}{2!}(x-7)^2 +\frac{g'''(7)}{3!}(x-7)^3+\cdots \end{equation*}
Substituting gives
\begin{equation*} g(x) = -3 - 4(x-7) + \frac{-4}{2!}(x-7)^2 +\frac{5}{3!}(x-7)^3+\cdots \end{equation*}
(a) The degree 2 Taylor polynomial, \(P_2 (x)\text{,}\) is obtained by truncating after the \((x-7)^2\) term:
\begin{equation*} P_2(x) = -3+\left(-4\right)\!\left(x-7\right)+\frac{1}{2!}\!\left(-4\right)\!\left(x-7\right)^{2}. \end{equation*}
(b) The degree 3 Taylor polynomial, \(P_3 (x)\text{,}\) is obtained by truncating after the \((x-7)^3\) term:
\begin{equation*} P_3 (x) = -3+\left(-4\right)\!\left(x-7\right)+\frac{1}{2!}\!\left(-4\right)\!\left(x-7\right)^{2}+\frac{1}{3!}\cdot 5\!\left(x-7\right)^{3} \end{equation*}
(c) Substitute \(x=6.9\) into the Taylor polynomial of degree 2:
\begin{equation*} P_2(4.9) = -3+\left(-4\right)\!\left(6.9-7\right)+\frac{1}{2!}\!\left(-4\right)\!\left(6.9-7\right)^{2} = -2.62. \end{equation*}
From the Taylor polynomial of degree 3, we obtain
\begin{equation*} P_3(4.9) = -3+\left(-4\right)\!\left(6.9-7\right)+\frac{1}{2!}\!\left(-4\right)\!\left(6.9-7\right)^{2}+\frac{1}{3!}\cdot 5\!\left(6.9-7\right)^{3} = -2.62083. \end{equation*}
